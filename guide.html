A Day in Sarah's Research

Follow a market researcher from raw survey data to boardroom-ready insights — and learn which method to use at every step.

Meet Sarah. She's a market researcher who just received 2,000 survey responses about a new product launch. Her stakeholders want answers by Friday. Here's how she uses statistical analysis to turn raw data into insights — and how you can too.

This tool is the market research companion to our UX Research Statistics Toolkit — built for UX researchers doing usability studies.

Sarah's Journey

Stage 1: Describe

"What does the data look like?"

Sarah starts where every good analyst starts — by getting a feel for the data. How many respondents completed the survey? What are the average satisfaction scores? Are there outliers that could skew her results? She runs descriptive statistics to build a baseline before diving deeper.

Stage 2: Compare

"Are customers in different regions responding differently?"

Sarah's boss asks: "Is customer satisfaction the same across all four regions?" She has satisfaction ratings (1–10) broken down by region. Time to compare groups and see if the differences are real or just noise in the data.

Methods for this
ANOVA t-test Kruskal-Wallis Mann-Whitney U Chi-Square Welch's ANOVA
Try it now
ANOVA Kruskal-Wallis T-test
How to decide

Comparing 2 groups? Use a t-test. Comparing 3+ groups? Use ANOVA. Data not normally distributed or ordinal? Use the non-parametric alternatives (Mann-Whitney for 2, Kruskal-Wallis for 3+). Comparing proportions? Chi-Square.

3
Stage 3: Correlate Stage 4: Predict

"What drives satisfaction?"

Now Sarah needs to find what predicts high satisfaction scores. Is it price perception? Brand trust? Ease of use? She starts by looking at how these variables relate to each other (correlate), then builds a model to identify which factors matter most (predict).

Methods for this
Pearson Correlation Spearman Correlation Multiple Regression Logistic Regression Partial Correlation
Try it now
Pearson Correlation Multiple Regression
How to decide

Exploring relationships? Start with correlation. Predicting a continuous outcome (like a score)? Use linear regression. Predicting a yes/no outcome (like churn)? Use logistic regression. Need to control for confounders? Partial correlation.

4
Stage 5: Segment

"Are there distinct customer types?"

Marketing wants to know if there are natural customer segments they can target differently. Sarah looks for clusters — groups of respondents who answered similarly — to build data-driven personas for the campaign team.

Methods for this
K-Means Clustering Latent Class Analysis Hierarchical Clustering Factor Analysis
Try it now
K-Means LCA
How to decide

Numeric data with known cluster count? K-Means. Binary or categorical survey responses? Latent Class Analysis. Want to see the hierarchy of how groups merge? Hierarchical clustering. Want to reduce many variables into a few dimensions first? Factor Analysis.

5
Stage 1: Describe (Reliability)

"Does our scale actually work?"

Before publishing her findings, Sarah needs to validate that her 8-item satisfaction scale is measuring what it's supposed to measure. Are the items internally consistent? Do they load onto the expected factors? This is about the quality of the instrument itself.

Methods for this
Cronbach's Alpha Exploratory Factor Analysis Confirmatory Factor Analysis Item Response Theory
Try it now
Cronbach's Alpha Factor Analysis
How to decide

Checking if items are consistent? Cronbach's Alpha. Discovering what structure your items have? EFA (exploratory). Confirming a hypothesized structure? CFA (confirmatory). Analyzing item difficulty and discrimination? IRT.

6

"Which product features matter most?"

The product team is debating which features to prioritize for v2. Sarah designs a preference study to find out which features drive purchase intent — and which trade-offs customers are actually willing to make.

Methods for this
Conjoint Analysis MaxDiff Discrete Choice

These preference methods are coming soon. In the meantime, explore the Learn page for interactive tutorials on related techniques.

How to decide

Ranking which items are most/least important? MaxDiff. Understanding trade-offs between product features (price vs. quality vs. speed)? Conjoint. Simulating realistic purchase decisions? Discrete Choice.

7

"Did the campaign work?"

Finally, Sarah needs to evaluate whether last month's marketing campaign actually moved the needle. Did the test group buy more than the control? Was it the campaign, or would it have happened anyway? She needs causal evidence, not just correlation.

Advanced methods
A/B Test Difference-in-Differences Propensity Score Matching Bayesian Test

Causal inference methods are advanced territory. Check the Learn page for guided walkthroughs when available.

How to decide

Ran a randomized experiment? A/B test. Observational data with a before/after and treatment/control? Difference-in-Differences. Need to create comparable groups from non-random data? Propensity Score Matching. Want probability of improvement instead of p-values? Bayesian test.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is my data safe?

Yes. Everything runs in your browser. No data is sent to any server.

What file formats work?

Just paste values from any spreadsheet — Excel, Google Sheets, CSV.

How many rows can it handle?

Up to 3,000 rows per column. Most methods run instantly up to 1,000 rows. Heavier methods (regression, factor analysis, clustering) may take a few seconds with 2,000-3,000 rows.

How many columns can I use?

Most methods work with 2-10 columns. Regression and factor analysis can handle up to 15-20 columns comfortably. Beyond 20 columns with 3,000 rows, some methods may slow down.

What about checkbox / multi-response questions?

Yes! If your survey has “select all that apply” questions, paste all the columns at once (tab-separated) into a single input box. The tool detects multiple columns automatically and analyzes selection patterns.

Can I run multiple analyses on the same data?

Yes. After each analysis, you’ll see a “What would you like to do next?” panel with options to run different methods without re-entering your data question.

How do I know which method to use?

Start with the guided conversation — answer a few plain questions and the tool picks the right method. Or read “A Day in Sarah’s Research” above for real-world scenarios. Power users can browse all 59+ methods directly.

Do I need to know statistics?

No. The tool guides you to the right method and explains results in plain language.
